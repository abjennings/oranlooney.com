The Epistemology of Computation

  • September 22, 2023
  • Philosophy  
  • Computer Science  

Sense data, or experience A priori knowledge or instincts

Locke thought there were three: intuitive, demonstrative, and sensitive, although he may have been kidding about demonstrative.

Critical mass example. You can know the all the relevant theory, you can have the data and measurements in front of you. You even know the exact steps you will take to carry out the calculations, to the extent that you could delegate that task to a fairly simple machine if you wanted to. But until you actually carry out those calculations, you don’t actually know how to build a working nuclear bomb.

Where does that knowledge come from? Not from sense data, and not from a priori knowledge; you had those already.

You can sit and stare at the beauty of the equations describing the standard model all day long, but if you actually want to predict anything you’ve got to sit down and crunch those numbers.

Feynman diagrams are basically a way to keep track of approximate solutions to those equations. They’re useful because they make it easier to compute; they don’t add anything fundamental to the theory.

An algorithm, by definition, requires no thought, no creativity. It is a clear and unambiguous set of steps. It’s just work: tedious work.

Hypothetico-deductive method assumes a model. Fitting a model is work and requires computation.

The fundamental problem of causal inference (FPCI) requires a model

Theory-ladenness of observation - of course you have a model, even if you don’t know it.

If sense data were all that was required, databases and file systems would be geniuses. But you can’t simply pile up facts; you have to process them, fit them into some model. That model can be as simple as a B+ tree index allowing only for retrieval, but it has to exist.

Indexing, compressing, organizing data creates knowledge.

Universe has a bound on how much computation can occur in a given region or for a given amount of energy.

Conventional computers use irreversible operations that increase entropy, but there are theoretical models of computation that use only reversible physics. Reversible computers seem pretty impractical to me and seem to require an infinite amount of precision and a great deal of work to set up.

That’s a puzzle, but it’s not a epistemology puzzle. For all practical purposes, the universe has plenty of computational capacity to spare, and we can make devices which capture this.
